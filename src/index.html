Who pays for higher education in Washington?

Over the last 20 years, as state appropriations for higher education went down, tuition rates increased. The numbers in these charts have been adjusted for inflation.

Washington colleges and universities have steadily collected more in tuition revenue over the last 20 years, but the amount they charged escalated sharply after the Great Recession as schools tried to make up for cuts in state appropriations. After a few years of double-digit tuition hikes, lawmakers put the brakes on the price of going to college in 2014, and capped the amount tuition could rise. These numbers also reflect an increase in full-time-equivalent enrollment of about 34% between 2000 and 2018. More students mean more tuition is collected.
Tuition went up after the 2008 recession to fill in for steep cuts in state appropriations. As the economic recovery took off, about five years ago, state legislators became more generous in their funding for colleges and universities.
Before the Great Recession, Washington’s public colleges and universities received the lion’s share of their funding from the state. Today, the majority of their funds come from tuition. If state legislators respond as they did in the 2008 recession and take a big chunk out of college funding to balance the budget, some colleges fear they won’t be able to make up the difference, especially if enrollments drop.
